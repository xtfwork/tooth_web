  • A look at dental X-ray radiation: Safety and recommendations

    Dental X-rays are safe and expose patients to low levels of radiation, even less than what an individual is exposed to in the environment.

    AI Summary

    The article titled "A Look at Dental X-ray Radiation: Safety and Recommendations" highlights the safety of dental X-rays, emphasizing they expose patients to low levels of radiation, even less than environmental exposure. Dr. Erika Benavides, a dentist from the University of Michigan School of Dentistry, chaired an ADA panel that published clinical safety and regulatory recommendations in 2024. Dental X-rays are essential for detecting oral health issues such as dental caries, cysts, tumors, gum disease, and bone loss. Despite the known DNA damage potential of ionizing radiation, dental X-rays expose patients to very low levels. The ADA and American Academy of Oral and Maxillofacial Radiology no longer recommend protective aprons or thyroid-protecting collars during X-rays. Both organizations claim that dental X-rays are safe for pregnant women and their fetuses, with exposure levels up to 30,000 times lower than harmful thresholds. For example, bitewing X-rays expose a patient to about five microsieverts, while panoramic X-rays expose them to about 20 microsieverts of radiation. The experts emphasized that these levels have significantly decreased compared to 30 years ago.

  • Occlusal appliance fabrication: Optical vs. conventional tracking devices

    Researchers have examined whether optical tracking devices could be more accurate compared with conventional tracking devices.

    AI Summary

    In a study published in the Journal of Prosthodontics, researchers compared optical tracking devices with conventional tracking devices for fabricating occlusal appliances. The research involved 12 patients over 25 years old. Three-dimensionally printed appliances were made using computer-aided design software and each utilizing one of three tracking methods: optical, conventional, or average articulator values. For static occlusion, the optical tracking devices showed lower mean discrepancy in contact points at maximal intercuspation position compared to both conventional devices and average articulator values. Both optical and conventional tracking devices also exhibited lower discrepancies than average articulator values at final protrusive position. Moreover, optical tracking devices significantly reduced anterior open bite at maximal intercuspation position compared with average articulator values. For dynamic occlusion, the optical tracking devices outperformed both other methods and demonstrated no interferences during protrusive movement in all subjects. The study suggested that using optical tracking devices could potentially improve treatment efficacy and clinical outcomes for patients, although further research may be needed to confirm these findings.
