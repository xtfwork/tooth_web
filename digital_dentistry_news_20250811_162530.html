  • Researchers learn from marine mollusks’ indestructible teeth, aim for industrial applications
    Published: 2025-08-11 13:06

    With advances in bioengineering, researchers are continually looking to nature for materials with exceptional properties. Their latest focus is on marine mollusks called chitons and The post Researchers learn from marine mollusks’ indestructible teeth, aim for industrial applications appeared first on Oral Health Group.

    AI summary

    A study led by researchers from the University of California, Irvine, in collaboration with Japanese universities, has discovered the biological process behind chitons' ability to grow teeth harder than human enamel. This groundbreaking research sheds light on the mechanisms of precise, nanoscale mineral formation within the teeth and presents opportunities for the development of environmentally friendly and sustainable materials such as batteries, fuel cell catalysts, semiconductors, and 3D printing. The team found that chiton-specific iron-binding proteins (RTMP1) are transported via nanoscale tubules into the forming teeth, where they bind with chitin nanofiber scaffolds and iron stored in ferritin. This binding guides the deposition of magnetite nanorods that give the teeth extraordinary hardness, stiffness, and magnetic properties. The research was published in Science and received funding from the U.S. Air Force Office of Scientific Research and the Army Research Office. Chitons are a type of marine mollusk, with over 900 species mostly found in intertidal coastal regions. The species studied in this research are larger and inhabit the northwest coast of the United States and off Hokkaido, Japan. This collaboration between biological and materials science has provided insights into one of Earth's hardest and strongest biological materials.

  • ChatGPT limited in answering complex oral health questions, study finds
    Published: 2025-08-11 01:14

    AMMAN, Jordan: As artificial intelligence continues to expand its role in healthcare, large language models such as ChatGPT are increasingly being used to ...

    AI summary

    In a study titled "Assessing ChatGPT's suitability in responding to the public's inquiries on the effects of smoking on oral health," researchers evaluated the effectiveness of ChatGPT 3.5 in providing reliable information about tobacco use and its impact on oral health. The team, consisting of members from the University of Jordan and the Kuwait Ministry of Health, generated a pool of 119 commonly asked questions related to periodontal conditions, teeth, oral hygiene, surgery, and other oral health concerns. Responses were assessed based on usefulness, readability, quality, reliability, and actionability. Most responses were deemed either very useful (36.1%) or useful (42.0%), of moderate to good quality (78.2%). However, only 23.5% of responses scored highly in terms of actionability and 35.3% were moderately easy to read. The tool's performance varied across topics, with less effective responses provided for specialized topics like the effects of tobacco use on oral soft tissue and oral surgeries. The study highlights that while ChatGPT is useful for providing general information about the impact of tobacco use on oral health, it faces challenges in readability, consistency, and quality when addressing more detailed questions or specialized topics. The authors emphasize that while this technology can supplement healthcare education, it should not replace professional dental advice. The study was published online on 19 July 2025 in BMC Oral Health.
